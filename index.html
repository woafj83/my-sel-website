Supporting Social-Emotional Learning in 3rd and 4th Grade

Students in grades 3 and 4 are building friendships, self-awareness, empathy, and emotional control while becoming more independent learners. This website helps educators connect child development research to practical classroom strategies and home-school partnerships.

Although 3rd and 4th graders are in middle childhood, their current social and emotional development is shaped by experiences that began before birth and continued through the early years of life.

Why Social-Emotional Learning Matters in 3rd and 4th Grade

By middle childhood, students are expected to manage stronger emotions, work in groups, follow multi-step directions, handle peer conflict, and recover from disappointment. SEL supports academic learning because students who feel safe, connected, and emotionally supported are better able to focus, persist, and participate.

How prenatal development and care can affect later social-emotional growth

  • Healthy prenatal care supports brain development before birth, which lays a foundation for later attention, emotional responses, and stress management.
  • Prenatal stress, poor nutrition, substance exposure, and limited medical care can increase risk for later difficulties with emotional regulation, attention, and behavior.
  • These factors do not determine a child’s future, but they can influence how much support the child may need as they grow.

How early childhood experiences affect adolescent social and emotional development

  • Warm, responsive caregiving helps children build trust, attachment, and a sense of safety.
  • Early opportunities to name emotions, solve conflicts, and practice routines strengthen self-control and social competence over time.
  • Chronic stress, trauma, inconsistent caregiving, or unmet developmental needs in the early years can make later peer relationships and emotional coping more difficult.
  • Protective relationships with adults at home and at school can reduce risk and promote resilience across childhood and adolescence.
Big idea for educators: A 3rd or 4th grader’s behavior often reflects a long developmental story. When teachers respond with structure, empathy, and skill-building, they help students strengthen the social and emotional tools they need now and in adolescence.

Three Developmental Stages That Matter for 3rd and 4th Grade SEL

The developmental stages below show how social-emotional growth unfolds over time. The final stage, middle childhood, is the stage most directly connected to 3rd and 4th grade students.

🤰

Prenatal Stage

Foundation stage: brain and body systems begin developing before birth.

Key social-emotional foundations

  • Development of the nervous system that later supports calming, attention, and stress responses.
  • Early sensitivity to maternal stress, health, and environmental conditions.
  • Beginnings of brain architecture that support future learning, behavior, and emotional responses.

Two classroom SEL strategies connected to this stage

1. Development matters mini-lesson: Teach students that everyone grows differently and that brains develop over time. This helps build empathy and reduces judgment about classmates’ behavior.
2. Strength-based language: Use phrases such as “skills grow with practice” and “your brain is learning a new strategy” to reinforce that development is ongoing and support can change outcomes.
🧒

Early Childhood

Approximate ages: 3 to 5 years

Social-emotional milestones

  • Begins to identify basic emotions in self and others.
  • Starts cooperative play and takes turns with support.
  • Uses words more often than actions to express wants and feelings.
  • Develops growing independence while still relying on adult co-regulation.

Two classroom SEL strategies for educators

1. Feelings check-in routine: Begin the day with an emotion chart or “How are you arriving today?” prompt so students practice naming feelings and noticing others’ emotions.
2. Role-play with puppets or scenarios: Use simple social situations such as sharing supplies, losing a game, or joining a group to model problem-solving and kind responses.
📚

Middle Childhood

Approximate ages: 8 to 11 years — includes most 3rd and 4th graders

Social-emotional milestones

  • Builds stronger peer relationships and greater concern about fairness and belonging.
  • Compares self to others and develops a stronger self-concept.
  • Uses more advanced perspective-taking and empathy.
  • Improves emotional control but still needs coaching during stress, conflict, or frustration.

Two classroom SEL strategies for 3rd and 4th grade

1. Collaborative problem-solving circles: Give students structured chances to discuss class challenges, listen to others, and generate fair solutions together.
2. Reflection journals: Have students respond to prompts such as “What challenged me today?” “How did I handle it?” and “What could I try next time?” to build self-awareness and emotion management.

What Is Self-Regulation?

Self-regulation is the ability to manage emotions, attention, behavior, and impulses in order to meet expectations or goals. In elementary classrooms, self-regulation helps students wait, listen, persist, calm down, recover from mistakes, and respond appropriately during social situations.

Examples of self-regulation in 3rd and 4th grade

  • A student feels angry during group work but uses a breathing strategy instead of yelling.
  • A child is disappointed by a grade and asks for help rather than shutting down.
  • A student wants to interrupt but raises a hand and waits for a turn.
  • A child notices feeling overwhelmed and asks for a short break before rejoining the task.

Why self-regulation matters

  • Supports learning by helping students focus and persist.
  • Improves peer relationships by reducing reactive behavior.
  • Builds independence and confidence over time.
  • Creates healthier responses to stress in later childhood and adolescence.

Ways teachers can model and encourage self-regulation

1. Model calm thinking out loud

Use statements such as, “I am feeling frustrated, so I’m going to pause and take a breath before I respond.” This shows students what regulation looks and sounds like.

2. Teach predictable calming routines

Create a class reset routine with breathing, stretching, quiet reflection, or a short sensory break. Repetition helps students use the routine independently.

3. Scaffold problem-solving

Use prompts like “What happened? What are you feeling? What do you need? What is one respectful next step?” so students learn how to pause before reacting.

Strategies Families Can Use at Home

When home and school use similar language and routines, children are more likely to develop strong self-regulation and social-emotional skills. The ideas below are organized by the same developmental stages.

Prenatal stage

  • Encourage healthy prenatal care, stress reduction, and supportive relationships for expecting caregivers whenever possible.
  • Share community resources for healthcare, nutrition, and mental health support with families in need.

Early childhood

  • Use everyday feeling words at home such as happy, worried, frustrated, calm, and proud.
  • Practice turn-taking games, bedtime routines, and visual schedules to build predictability and emotional security.

Middle childhood

  • Hold short family check-ins where children can share one success, one challenge, and one goal.
  • Help children solve social problems with questions such as “What happened?” “How did the other person feel?” and “What could you do next time?”
Home-school connection for 3rd and 4th grade: Teachers can send home a monthly SEL tip sheet with one calming strategy, one conversation starter, and one family activity such as gratitude journaling, emotion charades, or goal-setting.

Takeaway for Educators

Social-emotional learning is not separate from academics. For 3rd and 4th graders, SEL is part of how students learn, build friendships, manage mistakes, and see themselves as capable members of a classroom community. Educators who understand the developmental roots of student behavior are better prepared to respond with empathy, structure, and intentional instruction.

In other words, when teachers build emotional vocabulary, teach problem-solving, model self-regulation, and partner with families, they support children not just for one school year, but for the next stages of development as well.
